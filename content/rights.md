# Know Your Rights: School Abuse and Title IX

<div class="aux-intro">
If you or your child was sexually abused at school, by a coach, or by any person in a position of institutional authority — do not assume your legal window has closed. Missouri and Illinois have both significantly extended the deadlines for childhood sexual abuse claims. Read this before concluding anything.
</div>

## Before You Do Anything: Do Not Self-Screen

The single most common reason survivors miss their legal window is that they assumed — without consulting an attorney — that it was too late. Laws in both Missouri and Illinois have been significantly expanded in recent years specifically to give survivors more time. An attorney experienced in these cases will do the SOL analysis. Your job is to make the call before you decide you can't.

---

## Statute of Limitations: Missouri

### Missouri Childhood Sexual Abuse — RSMo 537.046

Missouri law gives survivors of childhood sexual abuse the **later** of two windows — meaning you use whichever one gives you more time:

1. **10 years after your 21st birthday** (until age 31), OR
2. **3 years after you discovered — or reasonably should have discovered — that the injury or condition you have was caused by childhood sexual abuse**

The second window — the discovery rule — is critical for adult survivors. Many people do not connect current psychological or emotional harm to childhood abuse until they are in therapy, until a family member discloses similar abuse, or until an institutional cover-up becomes public. When that connection is made, the 3-year clock on the discovery window may not have started yet — regardless of when the abuse occurred.

**If you are under 31:** You likely still have a claim under the age window regardless of when the abuse occurred.

**If you are over 31:** You may still have a claim under the discovery rule. When did you first connect your current condition to the childhood abuse? That date — not the date of the abuse — is what starts the discovery clock.

**Minority tolling (RSMo 516.170):** Missouri law suspends the running of any SOL while the plaintiff is a minor. The clock does not start until age 18.

### Title IX Civil Claims in Missouri

- Federal Title IX civil action: generally 5 years (borrowing Missouri's personal injury SOL, RSMo 516.120)
- State law negligence (negligent hiring, negligent supervision, negligent retention): 5 years (RSMo 516.120), tolled during minority
- Missouri childhood sexual abuse (RSMo 537.046): see above — 10 years from age 21 or 3 years from discovery, whichever is later

## Statute of Limitations: Illinois

### Illinois Childhood Sexual Abuse — 735 ILCS 5/13-202.2

Illinois has **no statute of limitations** for civil claims arising from childhood sexual abuse when the plaintiff was under 18 at the time of the abuse. There is no deadline. A survivor can bring a civil claim for childhood sexual abuse at any point in their life.

This applies to:
- Sexual abuse by a teacher, coach, counselor, administrator, or other school employee
- Abuse that occurred at a school, on a school trip, or in any school-related context
- Claims against the individual abuser and claims against the institution for negligent hiring, supervision, or retention

The no-SOL rule reflects the Illinois legislature's recognition that childhood sexual abuse causes harm that may not be understood or connected to its source until years or decades later.

### Title IX Civil Claims in Illinois

- Federal Title IX civil action: generally 2 years (borrowing Illinois personal injury SOL, 735 ILCS 5/13-202)
- State law childhood sexual abuse: **no SOL** (see above)
- Note: the state law no-SOL claim is broader and should be analyzed alongside any federal claim

## The OCR Complaint: 180 Days — File Immediately

The U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights (OCR) investigates schools that violated Title IX. Filing is free. The deadline is **180 days from the discriminatory act** and does not extend for any reason — not school investigations, not criminal proceedings, not therapy.

Filing an OCR complaint does not prevent a civil lawsuit. It creates a public investigation record that can produce critical documentary evidence for the civil case. File both tracks simultaneously if you are within 180 days.

**File online:** [ocrcas.ed.gov](https://ocrcas.ed.gov)

---

## What Records Establish Institutional Knowledge

Winning a civil Title IX case requires showing that an "appropriate person" — a principal, superintendent, or Title IX coordinator — had actual knowledge of the abuse and responded with deliberate indifference. That evidence lives in the school's own records:

- Prior complaints about the same adult in the school's files
- Personnel records documenting investigations, warnings, and transfers
- School board executive session minutes — "personnel matters" discussions near the date of known abuse incidents
- Title IX coordinator correspondence and investigation files
- HR records showing when the school learned of the abuse

**Public records that establish patterns:**
- Missouri DESE educator certification disciplinary actions — available at [dese.mo.gov](https://dese.mo.gov/educator-quality/educator-certification)
- Illinois ISBE educator licensure records — [isbe.net](https://www.isbe.net/Pages/Educator-Licensure-Information-System.aspx)
- MSHSAA coaching assignment records — a coach at four schools in six years is a red flag
- U.S. DOE OCR complaint database — prior findings against the same district establish a pattern of deliberate indifference

## When to Act

**Recent abuse (within 180 days):** File an OCR complaint at [ocrcas.ed.gov](https://ocrcas.ed.gov) immediately. Consult a civil attorney simultaneously — do not wait for OCR to resolve.

**Abuse that happened years ago — Missouri:** Do not assume you are barred. If you are under 31, the age window is likely still open. If you are over 31, the discovery rule may still apply. Talk to a Missouri attorney who handles childhood sexual abuse claims.

**Abuse that happened years ago — Illinois:** There is no deadline. You can file a civil claim now.

**Never self-screen.** The analysis of whether a claim is timely is legal work. If you are asking whether it is too late, the answer you need comes from an attorney, not from this page.
